The situation

Forty years of extraordinary work. A website that couldn't show any of it.

Seacourt Print Workshop is one of Ireland's most respected printmaking studios — an open-access workshop, education centre, residency programme, and gallery that has shaped artists and communities in Bangor since 1981.

They had beautiful photography. Stunning finished artwork. A studio full of character and process documentation that any arts organisation would be proud of. But when a new artist, funder, or curious visitor arrived at their website, almost none of it was visible. The site was a static list of information with no visual hierarchy, no atmosphere, and no ability to let imagery breathe.

"The photographs were there. The artwork was there. The problem wasn't the assets — it was a website incapable of doing them justice. People were leaving before they'd understood what Seacourt was."

Why we were brought in

Remarkable photography and artwork, trapped behind a website that couldn't present either.

Seacourt isn't a single-audience organisation. Practising artists looking for studio access and residencies, community members wanting to learn, school groups, exhibition visitors, funders and Arts Council reviewers — they all needed to understand Seacourt in completely different ways.

None of them were being served. A flat, undifferentiated site was failing every one of them. And critically: Seacourt had years of genuinely beautiful photography and artwork documentation that the website had no mechanism to show.

Great imagery trapped behind a poor website

Seacourt had extraordinary photography and artwork. The old site had no visual hierarchy, no gallery capability, no ability to let an image breathe. All that material was invisible to anyone who visited.

No coherent visual identity

Four decades of growth, no consistent brand. Printed materials, digital, signage and social media all looked like they came from different organisations.

Five audiences, one undifferentiated navigation

Residency artists, community class members, school groups, exhibition visitors and funders were all pointed to the same pages. Nobody found what they were looking for quickly.

Funder and press visibility was weak

Arts Council reviewers and potential funders couldn't quickly understand the organisation's reach, impact or ambition. The site gave them nothing to engage with.

Session times under 90 seconds

New visitors were landing and leaving before understanding what Seacourt was. No atmosphere, no welcome, no reason to stay and explore.

The photography and artwork they already had

This is what existed. All of it was being wasted.

The response

A brand and a website built to finally do justice to the work already happening inside.

Every decision was anchored in what made Seacourt genuinely distinctive. Not imposed from outside — drawn from forty years of making, teaching, and community.

01

Brand identity

No coherent visual language across touchpoints. Materials, digital and signage contradicted each other. Nothing felt like it came from the same organisation.

A new identity rooted in the physicality of printmaking — ink bleed, registration marks, layered colour. Bold enough for a contemporary arts context. Warm enough to reflect the community at its heart. Applied consistently across print, digital, signage and social.

02

Website redesign

One undifferentiated site failing five different audiences simultaneously. No structure, no atmosphere, no gallery functionality. Session times under 90 seconds.

Rebuilt around five distinct user journeys — artists, community members, school groups, exhibition visitors, and funders/press. Full image galleries with proper visual space. A site that finally feels like a preview of the studio: considered, unhurried, full of craft.

03

Showcasing existing imagery

Seacourt had years of extraordinary photography and artwork. The old site had no gallery capability, no visual hierarchy, and no ability to let any of it breathe. All of it was invisible to anyone who visited.

We built the platform their existing photography and artwork deserved — full-screen galleries, exhibition pages with proper image presentation, artist profiles with visual space to let the work speak. The assets were already there. We built the frame.

04

Content strategy & copywriting

No framework for telling the organisation's story. Courses, residencies, exhibitions, community work, and funding information were all equally weighted — which meant none of it stood out.

A content architecture that puts the work and the people front and centre. Artist profiles, process documentation, exhibition records, programme stories. Copy written to feel as considered as the prints themselves — and structured so the Seacourt team can maintain it confidently.

Exhibitions & residencies

A programme that attracts artists from across Ireland and beyond.

The residency programme brings established and emerging artists into the studio for extended periods. The exhibition programme has hosted work by major figures including Joe Tilson. The community programme reaches schools, groups, and individuals across North Down.

None of this was communicated clearly on the old site. Funders and press couldn't see the ambition of the programme. Prospective residents couldn't easily understand the opportunity. Visitors had no previews of what they might find on the walls.

The new site changed all of that — with dedicated exhibition pages, a structured residency section, and programme stories that give each strand of the work the space it deserves.

"For the first time, when someone visits the website, they understand what we are before they've read a word. The photography was always there — now it works for us."

Emma Drury, Director, Seacourt Print Workshop
