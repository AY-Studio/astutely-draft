Camborne
Town Council

A new digital presence for Cornwall's largest parish — 23,000 residents, 18 councillors, and a town with a great deal happening in it.

Client

Camborne Town Council

Location

Camborne, Cornwall

Services

Web Design & Build, Content Architecture, Digital Accessibility

Sector

Local Government & Public Sector

The situation

Cornwall's largest parish. A town with serious momentum. A website that wasn't keeping pace with any of it.

Camborne is geographically the largest parish in Cornwall — nearly 23,000 residents, 18 elected councillors, six managed green spaces, a library, a packed events calendar, and £23.7 million in Town Deal funding actively transforming the town.

Trevithick Day draws tens of thousands. The Great Big Green Fest fills the recreation ground every summer. The council runs programmes for youth, the environment, allotments, community safety, and Fairtrade. And it does all of this from the striking Passmore Edwards Building — a Victorian civic landmark that has housed local democracy since 1985.

The old website served none of that ambition. Static, hard to navigate, and unable to communicate to the diverse range of people who needed to interact with it — residents, businesses, community groups, press, funders, and visitors to the town.

"Listen, Improve, Achieve. The council knew what it stood for. The website just wasn't saying it."

The town in full

A community with real life in it — and a digital presence to match.

Events, governance, parks, projects, history — all of it needed a home that could communicate clearly to residents, visitors, funders, and press.

Why we were brought in

A public sector website serves everyone — or it fails everyone.

A town council website has an unusual brief: it must work equally well for a 70-year-old resident checking bin collection days, a journalist covering the mayor, a parent looking for summer holiday activities, a business applying for a grant, and a Cornwall Council officer reviewing planning responses.

Serving all of them through a single site — accessibly, clearly, and without making anyone feel like they've landed in the wrong place — demands careful content architecture and a platform that can handle complexity without showing it.

Too many audiences, one undifferentiated front door. Residents, visitors, businesses, community groups, funders, press, and councillors all arriving at the same homepage with no clear path to what they needed.

Accessibility requirements unmet. As a public body, Camborne TC has legal obligations under WCAG accessibility standards. The old site fell short — and risked excluding the very residents most in need of council services.

£23.7m Town Deal projects with no proper digital home. Three major regeneration projects — AWENVA, Park Gerry, and Holman Gardens — needed visibility, progress updates, and public engagement that a static site couldn't support.

Events generating 60,000+ views with nowhere to land. Trevithick Day footage went viral on social media. That traffic had no quality destination — nothing to convert curiosity into civic engagement or repeat visits.

Governance transparency hard to find. Council minutes, budget summaries, planning decisions, committee reports — all required by law to be publicly accessible, none of them easy to locate.

What we did

A website structured around people — not around how the council organises itself internally.

01

Website redesign & rebuild

The existing site was static, undifferentiated, and unable to reflect the energy or ambition of a council managing six parks, a library, multiple events programmes, and £23.7m of regeneration work simultaneously.

A fully rebuilt WordPress site with a content system the council team can manage confidently — no developer required. Clean, modern, and built to reflect the character of the town: accessible, civic, and proud of Camborne's history. Structured so that residents find what they need in seconds and funders see the full picture of what the council delivers.

02

Content architecture & navigation

Five distinct audiences sharing one navigation structure meant nobody found what they needed quickly. The site was organised around how the council worked, not around how residents and visitors think.

A rethought information architecture with clear audience pathways: residents, businesses, community groups, visitors, and the governance/transparency section for press and funders. Events, news, parks, governance, services, and opportunities all given clear homes with logical hierarchy. Designed to scale as the council's activity continues to grow.

03

Accessibility & digital inclusion

Public sector websites are required to meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards. The old site didn't meet them — creating a legal risk and, more importantly, excluding the residents who most need access to council services.

Full WCAG 2.1 AA compliance built into the redesign from the start. Integration of the Recite Me assistive toolbar — providing screen reader support, text customisation, translation tools, and reading aids directly in the browser. Bilingual English/Cornish content supported throughout. The most accessible council website in the area.

£23.7 million Town Deal

Three major regeneration projects — each one needed its own digital presence within the site.

The Camborne Town Deal is one of the largest investments in the town's history. The council's three projects needed their own pages: explaining the vision, tracking progress, and giving residents a place to engage with what's being built in their town.

AWENVA

Converting The Basset Centre and White House into a vibrant community hub — arts, wellbeing, enterprise, and community in one building in the heart of Camborne.

Park Gerry Community Fields

Transforming underutilised playing fields into a community hub for play, sport and leisure — with new facilities designed around what residents actually asked for.

Holman Gardens

A brownfield site transformed into an attractive gateway to the town centre — public garden, amphitheatre, walking paths, and seating for community events.

The scope

From a resident checking bin days to a journalist covering the mayor's speech, from a funder reviewing the council's impact to a parent signing up for a Trevithick Day event — the same website has to serve all of them, on any device, in any language, with any level of visual or cognitive ability.
