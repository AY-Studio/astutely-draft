# LinkedIn Posts — Astutely

Tone: direct, no filler, written from experience. No hooks, no listicle openers, no emoji storms.
Cadence: one per week is plenty. Quality over volume.

---

## 01 — On why projects fail

Most digital projects fail in definition, not execution.

The brief was vague. The strategy was assumed. The team weren't aligned on what success looked like.

Before we design, build or market anything, we get specific: what does this actually need to do? For whom? What does "working" look like in twelve months?

It takes longer upfront. It saves weeks — sometimes the whole project — on the back end.

## 02 — On positioning

If your marketing team can't explain what you do in one sentence — without using words like "solutions", "innovative" or "passionate" — you have a positioning problem, not a marketing problem.

Most fragmented marketing is a clarity problem in disguise.

Fix the foundation. The tactics will follow.

## 03 — On agency model

The pitch is done by the partners. The work is done by the graduates.

This is so normalised in agency land that clients have stopped expecting anything different.

We've deliberately stayed small. The people in the room during the brief are the people building the work. No re-briefing. No quality drop between promise and delivery.

It means we take fewer clients at a time. That's the point.

## 04 — On email automation (the right kind)

Everyone talks about abandoned basket emails.

The more interesting automation is event-triggered — based on what's about to happen in your world, not what the user hasn't done in their cart.

One of our clients sends a sequence timed around competition end dates: 72 hours out, 24 hours, 2 hours. Not because it's clever, but because that's when their audience is paying attention. The open rates reflect it.

Automation works when it's built around your audience's calendar, not yours.

## 05 — On websites that don't convert

A website that doesn't convert isn't usually a design problem.

It's a clarity problem. Visitors can't work out:
— What you actually do
— Whether it's for them
— What to do next

Design can't rescue copy that doesn't answer those three questions. Strategy has to come first.

---

## 06 — On narrative drift

Growing companies lose their story.

Not suddenly — gradually. A new service here, a new market there, a rebrand that was rushed, a website that hasn't been touched in three years.

The story becomes inconsistent. Marketing feels disjointed. Sales takes longer. Clients aren't sure what you stand for.

The fix is almost never more content. It's almost always more clarity about what you're actually saying and to whom.

## 07 — On discovery

Two days.

That's what we ask for before any major project starts — not to workshop in circles or produce a slide deck nobody reads, but to get the brief right.

Positioning, audience, message, success criteria. Clear, documented, agreed.

Projects that start with that foundation finish faster, stay on budget more often, and produce better work. It's not complicated. It just requires the discipline to slow down before speeding up.

---

## 08 — On sports clubs and content

Sports clubs have better raw content than most consumer brands.

Real emotion. Real stories. Fans who genuinely care. Matches, signings, behind-the-scenes — the kind of moments that most brands spend fortunes trying to manufacture.

The problem is rarely the content. It's that the infrastructure isn't there to capture, organise and distribute it consistently.

Build the system. The content will work harder than any campaign you could buy.

---

## 09 — On traffic as a goal

"We want more traffic" is usually the wrong brief.

More traffic to a page that doesn't convert just gives you more data about your problem.

The better questions: who specifically do we want more of, and what do we want them to do when they get here? Work backwards from that, and the right traffic source becomes obvious.

---

## 10 — On good client relationships

The best client relationships we have are the ones where the client pushes back.

Not obstructive — informed. They've thought deeply about their business. They challenge our thinking. They come back with "but what about this?" The work is better for it every time.

The brief we're handed is rarely the real brief. Good agency work is the process of getting to it together.

---

## 11 — On measurement

We ask every new client the same question before we start: what would have to be true in twelve months for this to have been worth it?

Most people haven't been asked that before. The answer — when they get to it — changes what we build entirely.

Vanity metrics are easy to hit. Make sure you're aiming at the right thing first.

---

## 12 — On brand consistency

Your brand isn't your logo.

It's every touchpoint someone has with your business — the website, the proposal, the email footer, the way the phone gets answered, what happens when something goes wrong.

Inconsistency at any of those points costs you trust. And trust, once eroded, is expensive to rebuild.
